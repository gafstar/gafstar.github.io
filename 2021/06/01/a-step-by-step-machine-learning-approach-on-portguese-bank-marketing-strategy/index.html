A Step by Step Machine Learning Approach on Porguese Bank Marketing Strategy

This data comes from UCI Machine Learning Repository. From analyzing this data, I believe there are some insights that can be applied to other companies strategy in acquiring new clients.

The data is related with direct telemarketing campaigns of a Portuguese banking institution. The marketing campaigns were based on phone calls. Often, more than one contact to the same client was required, in order to access if the phone call will result for a long term deposit or not. This analysis is based only on 10% of the overall dataset used by S. Moro et al., 2014.

[Moro et al., 2014] S. Moro, P. Cortez and P. Rita. A Data-Driven Approach to Predict the Success of Bank Telemarketing. Decision Support Systems, Elsevier, 62:22-31, June 2014

A free copy of the paper can be found in the following link.

Note: Throughout the post, I have used the word subscribe often to denote if the phone call would result in long term deposit. The converses is true as well, not subscribe would refer to the chances of a phone call will not result in long term deposit.

First let's break down the some the terminologies.

What is marketing strategy?¶

Marketing strategy is a long-term, forward-looking approach to planning with the fundamental goal in sustainable advantage. Or simply put, it is a business plan for reaching people and turning them into customers of the business product or services.

What is marketing campaign?

Marketing campaing is the process of promoting a product or service with different media, including but not limited to newspapers, tv, online platforms like social media. Marketing campaign is one piece of overall marketing strategy executed with particular goal. A successful marketing campaign comprises of many factors. Some of them are:

  1. Knowing how your marketing fits into your marketing plan
  2. Setting marketing campaign's objective and parameters
  3. Metrics for measuring success
  4. Setting marketing campaign budget
  5. Choosing marketing strategy to communicate with customers
  6. Creating timeline and Action plan

What is deposit?

Deposit is a financial term that has multiple meanings. In this refers to the transaction of funds to another party (i.e Banks) with a fixed rate for safekeeping and being able to withdraw it at maturity time. In other words deposit is the money that a customer transfers into the saving or checking account held in a bank.

Now we have defined what marketing strategy and deposit means, let's start looking at the data to get some insight before we apply machine learning.

Prelimenary data analysis¶

Data Description¶

The data consists of 17 mixed (categorical and numeric) features with no missing value.

Bank client data:

  • age (numeric)
  • job : type of job (categorical: 'admin.','blue-collar','entrepreneur','housemaid','management','retired','selfemployed','services','student','technician','unemployed','unknown')
  • marital : marital status (categorical: 'divorced','married','single','unknown'; note: 'divorced' means divorced or widowed)
  • education (categorical: 'basic.4y','basic.6y','basic.9y','high.school','illiterate','professional.course','university.degree','unknown')
  • default: has credit in default? (categorical: 'no','yes','unknown')
  • housing: has housing loan? (categorical: 'no','yes','unknown')
  • loan: has personal loan? (categorical: 'no','yes','unknown')
  • Related with the last contact of the current campaign:
  • contact: contact communication type (categorical: 'cellular','telephone')
  • month: last contact month of year (categorical: 'jan', 'feb', 'mar', ..., 'nov', 'dec')
  • day_of_week: last contact day of the week (categorical: 'mon','tue','wed','thu','fri')
  • duration: last contact duration, in seconds (numeric). Important note: this attribute highly affects the output target (e.g., if duration=0 then y='no'). Yet, the duration is not known before a call is performed. Also, after the end of the call y is obviously known. Thus, this input should only be included for benchmark purposes and should be discarded if the intention is to have a realistic predictive model.

Other attributes:

  • campaign: number of contacts performed during this campaign and for this client (numeric, includes last contact)
  • pdays: number of days that passed by after the client was last contacted from a previous campaign (numeric; 999 means client was not previously contacted)
  • previous: number of contacts performed before this campaign and for this client (numeric)
  • poutcome: outcome of the previous marketing campaign (categorical: 'failure','nonexistent','success')

Output variable (desired target):

  • y has the client subscribed a term deposit? (binary: 'yes','no')

Feature importance¶

Knowing how the variables affect the target variable is a key to understanding the data and building a good model. This process is called feature importance analysis. It is often used as part of the exploratory data analysis where it helps identify which variables have greater influence on the whether the phone call will result in long term deposit or not. It helps us answer questions like 'what makes a potential customer sign up for long term deposit or what are the characteristics of customers who don't sign up for long term deposit. Analyzing such questions at the beginning could gives some insight into factors affecting customer decision. We have different kinds of data types. Each has a different way of measuring feature importance, so first let's start looking at the categorical variables and then we will continue with numerical features.

For each categorical variable, we can look at the long term deposit rate. This would give us the breakdown by the category values. For example, let's consider having how having defaulted on credit affects subscribing to term deposit.

Risk ratio¶

In addition to looking the difference between group subscription rate and the global rate, it is interesting to look at the ratio between them. In statistics, the ratio between the probabilities of differing groups is called risk ratio.

risk = group rate / global rate

For example let's see the risk ratio of not having a housing loan: For "housing == no", the risk of not subscribing is 1.42.

risk = 0.17 / 0.12 = 1.42

The result, risk number, is between 0 and infinity. It also have a nice interpretation in terms of likelihood. If the difference between the group rate and global rate is close to zero, then the risk ratio is close to 1. For example, for customers with no housing loan had 42% times the risk of not making long term deposit than the global rate.

If the group rate and global rate are almost equal, then they have the same risk level. However, if the risk number is less than one, we say there low risk level.

However, in this example we are going to encode the categorical features with one-hot-encoding before we proceed to training the model.

One-hot encoding¶

When working with machine learning models, we just can't take and put categorical variables in the model, because models can only deal with numbers in matrices. So we need to encoode the categorical variables to numbers.

One such technique used for encoding is one-hot encoding.

This approach has the advantage of not weighing a value improperly as it coverts each category value in a new column and assigning it 1 or 0 (T/F) value to the column. For instance, the variable marital has values (single, married and divorced), we can represent a custoemr with married marital status as (0,1,0). In this case, the married marital status is active or hot, so it gets 1, while the remaining are not active or cold, so they are 0.

Build Models¶

We don't know which algorithms would be good on this problem or what configuration to use for our model. We try a bunch of algorithms and see which one gives us the best result. Most of the time the type of algorithm we pick depends on the data. For example if we are dealing with incomplete or missing data, tree based models tend to do well compared to other algorithms which are sensitive to missing values. Also, some models may be robust if our data contains outliers. We will see a mixture of two linear (LR and LDA) and nonlinear (KNN, CART, NB an SVM) algorithms. One of the most important aspects of building a machine learning model is choosing evaluation metric. We have to weigh in different elements of the model to come up with the best evaluation criteria. For instance, in this example, what would be a good metric for the phone call prediction to sell a long term deposits? We can either choose accuracy or f1-score to evaluate our model, however, we have to know when to use either of them. Accuracy is used when True Positives and True Negatives are more important, while f1-score is used when False Negatives and False Positives are important.

Accuracy is one of the simplest metrics to check how well your model did. It tells what percentage of the values can the classifier predict correctly. Companies use models with high accuracy, because cost of errors can be very big, but optimizing for model accuracy mitigates the cost. We can see that we got an accuracy of 0.9 or 90%. At a glance this might seem promising. However, when we look closely, the model is just predicting the majority class. Since we have around 90% of the data as majority class, our model can get 90% accuracy by simply predicting the majority class. If the model is then tested on a dataset with majority class being around 70%, then the test accuracy would dramatically drop down to around 60%. Accuracy is based on the assumption that the number of observation for each class is the same. If you have unbalanced number of observation for each class, the accuracy would be closely equal to the proportion of the majority class. Classification accuracy for an imbalanced class may give us a false sense of achieving high accuracy. Usually my-go-to metric for binary classification of imbalanced class is f1-score or PR AUC.

Beside accuracy, the confusion matrix is quite a popular metric. This is the one you should always look at besides accuracy.

Precision and recall are two extremely useful metrics from the confusion matrix. Let's see what precision and recall can tell us about the dataset.

1) Precision: implies the measure of correctly identified positive cases from all the predicted positive cases. Out of all the phone calls that were predicted to sell long term deposits, what percentage of that actually sold long term deposits? 

precision_score(y_true=y_val, y_pred=predictions)

0.6190476190476191

So, in this case we have 62% chance of correctly identifying the positive cases of a call resulting in a long term deposits. This doesn't look great given we have an accuracy of 90%.This is a good indicator of the fact that higher accuracy doesn't necessarily mean better model. Let's see the other metric recall if it tells us something better.

2) Recall: implies the measure of the correctly identified positive case from all of the actual positive cases. Out of all the phone calls that resulted in long term deposits, what percentage of them the model correctly predicted the result of a phone call to sell long term deposits? This will become a bit clearer when we see the metrics individually on the raw data. 

recall_score(y_true=y_val, y_pred=predictions)

0.36835891381345925

The recall of the model is 37%, which is worse than we expected given the accuracy being 90%. This is telling us that out of all customers who made long term deposits, the model was able to correctly identify 37% of them. This result underscores that we should not rely only on accuracy when we are evaluating classification.

Handling Class Imbalance¶

Most of the time the when dealing with classification predictive modeling, we have unequal observations for each class label. This poses a challenge because most machine learning algorithms are build under the assumption that the target classes have equal number of observations for each class. However, more often than not the distribution of the observation is across the known classes is biased or skewed. For instance, if we are working with fraud detection, we will likely have a the fraud or target class (positive class) as a minority class. So we have to some way of mitigating the class imbalance. There are several machine learning techniques that address this problem. In this post we are going to see only one technique called Random Oversampling. It involves randomly selecting examples from the minority class, and adding them to the training dataset, so that the training dataset will end up having about equal number of examples for each class.

Conclusion¶

In this post we analyzed the Portuguese Bank Marketing Strategy data. We explored feature importance, analyzed risk ratio, built machine learning models and we selected the best mode and finally we made some test predictions on a couple customers. We can expand on this by showing how to handle the missing values other than NAN's. Because job, education, contact, poutcome contain unknown and nonexistent as missing values. The easiest way to deal with missing values is to discard them, however, this might not be a wise idea as we don't want to throw away information that might be helpful for our analysis. A better option would be to impute them using mean, median or mode in place of each column. A more advanced way of handling missing values would be to impute them using machine learning techniques. This may sound as meta, to impute missing values by machine learning and then use machine learning on the imputed dataset. The other aspect that is worth considering while building machine learning models is class imbalance. We tried to oversampling technique to increase the values of the minority class (target class). In the above example we saw it increased the f1-score which is great. It might be worthwhile to try out other techniques as well.

  1. Use better algorithms like Xgboost and RandomForest. Usually tree based algorithms perform better than other traditional machine learning algorithms on dataset that has mising values.
  2. Impute missing values that contain unknown and nonexistent
  3. Improve by feature engineering
  4. Use ensemble methods, which is synonymous by wisdom of the crowd, as they generally give better result
  5. Add more dataset
