What is Cohort Analysis?

June 22, 2021 in Analytics
cohort_analysis

In this post you will discover:

  • What cohort analysis is.
  • Why cohort is useful.
  • Stages of performing proper cohort analysis.
  • A step by step cohort analysis of an e-Commerce data using python.

First, let's begin by defining what cohort mean. The word cohort is derived from Latin cohors meaning an enclosure or enclosed group. In digital analytics context it means a group of customers that share something in common. This refers to a groupo of users who have similar characteristics or experiences within a defined time-span. Therefore, cohort analysis is a type of behavioral analytics that breaks the dataset into groups before conducting analysis. The grouping metric depends on the question you want to answer to. Cohort analysis is very popular in e-commerce, SaaS and Gaming platforms. It helps companies see patterns clearly across time, hence, a company can adapt and tailor it service to those specific cohorts. Time span is an important part of the analysis. We want to understand how the group behaves as time progresses. We can use different levels of time granularity, however, weekly and monthly are two most common time spans used in cohort analysis.

In e-Commerce or SaaS, a company may only be interested in customers who churned a week after making their first purchase, which is an example specific of cohort analysis. A software developer may only care about new user sign ups after upgrading the user interface for sign up form. A company may want to assess if users coming via referral from certain advertizing platform are making more sign ups or purchases. This would help them identify which user interface change or marketing strategy is making impacts.

Why is cohort useful?¶

Cohort Analysis is beneficial for a company to that rely on long-term user engagment through customer relationship manager CRM. Here are some ways cohort analysis can be used.

  1. Understanding business cycles: It helps to proactively plan for customer aqcuisition or customer behaviour.
  2. Experimenting: It can be used with experimenting a cross-section study like A/B test so that helps you optimize your study. For example, if you run an A/B tst for a feature change by hypothesizing that blue layout (A group) causes more users to convert than a green layout (B group). You discover that that blue layout indeed causes more sign up than a green layout. Let's say you decide to do cohort analysis to see if those changes reflect across all geographies. You segment your user by month and zipcode and notice that for some regions the result was inconclusive. You will then investigate further as to why would be the case.
  3. Learn about user shopping habits: This will help understand what are the attributes for user/customer who come to the site for the first time? or how are new user/customers different from returning customers? For instance, for eCommerce companies, you may dive in to see the quantity of purchases made, revenue from those items, types of items...etc to learn the shopping habits.
  4. Helps you understand customer retention and/or churn: cohort analysis is very handy to analyse customer churn or retention. This post is dedicated to cohort analysis on user retention for an online retail company.
  5. Calculate customer lifetime value: Because, you don't know how long each relationship with a customer will be. Therefore, depending how long each relationship will be, you can make a good estimate and state CLV as a periodic value. For example, the cohort analysis on this post is about 13 months long, therefore, we can say "this customers 3-month CLV is \$XXXX".
  6. Provides actionable information. Having the cohorts information allows the ability to drill down to the users of specific cohorts at specific time period to gain better understanding of their behavior. Each new group provides the opportunity to start fresh set of users which allows us to look at only the data that is relevant to the specific question.

In order to perform a proper cohort analysis, there are four stages:¶

  1. Determine what question you want to answer: The main goal of doing cohort analysis is to come up with actionable information to act on to improve the business, user experience, revenue, retention, customer acquisition ...ect. To ensure that you have have to ask teh right question
  2. Define the metrics that will be able to help you answer the question. A proper cohort analysis requires the identification of an event, such us a user checking out, and specific events like total quatity or purchase value of each item.
  3. Define the specific cohort that are relevant. You can either analyse all the users and target them or perform attribute contribution in order to find the relevant differences between each of them and ultimately discover and explain their behavior as specific cohort. For instance, you can split users as unique or repeat as each group differ in actions, pricing, subscription type. Repeat buyers or premium subscribers might be a large chunk of a company's revenue than first time basics users. Therefore, in such cases, performing split cohort analysis might give you an insight into customer spending in relation to the company revenue.
  4. Perform the cohort analysis. You can use data visualization like line charts or heatmaps that will to understand your analysis.

A step by step cohort analysis of an e-Commerce data¶

We will use a dataset downloaded from UCI Machine Learning Repository . This repository contains different types of open source dataset.

Data Set Information:¶

This is a transnational data set which contains all the transactions occurring between 01/12/2010 and 09/12/2011 for a UK-based and registered non-store online retail.The company mainly sells unique all-occasion gifts. Many customers of the company are wholesalers.

Attribute Information:¶

  • InvoiceNo Invoice number. Nominal, a 6-digit integral number uniquely assigned to each transaction. If this code starts with letter 'c', it indicates a cancellation.
  • StockCode Product (item) code. Nominal, a 5-digit integral number uniquely assigned to each distinct product.
  • Description Product (item) name. Nominal.
  • Quantity The quantities of each product (item) per transaction. Numeric.
  • InvoiceDate Invice Date and time. Numeric, the day and time when each transaction was generated.
  • UnitPrice Unit price. Numeric, Product price per unit in sterling.
  • CustomerID Customer number. Nominal, a 5-digit integral number uniquely assigned to each customer.
  • Country Country name. Nominal, the name of the country where each customer resides.

Moving on with our data preparation, we will create two additional new features from the InvoiceDate field. InvoiceDate data type is datetime, however, we will create two new features InvoiceMonth and CohortMonth as date fields. 

# extract the month from the datetime field and create two new columns InvoiceMonth and CohortMonth
def get_month(x):
    return dt.datetime(x.year, x.month, 1)
df['InvoiceMonth'] = df['InvoiceDate'].apply(get_month)
df['CohortMonth'] = df.groupby('CustomerID')['InvoiceMonth'].transform('min')

In the above script, we created a function that return the year, month and first day. Then we created a new feature InvoiceMonth which returns the 

# extracting integer values for year, month and day
# this int values will be used in calculating a new 'CohortIndex' field.

def get_month_int(df, column):
    year = df[column].dt.year
    month = df[column].dt.month
    day = df[column].dt.day
    return year, month, day

## the _ below is just a placeholder which is used for variables that are temporary or insignificant

# create cohort index

Retention Rate Table¶

The "retention rate" in online transactions can be defined as the number of customers who continue to make purchases within a certain time window. In our case, this is an indicator that people continued to buy goods from December 2010 to December 2011, expressed as the percentage of existing customers who were still loyal from December 2010 to December 2011. It's great to have 100% customer retention rate. This shows that customers have a strong loyalty to your product. However, it is difficult to achieve 100% in e-Commerce as customers are bombarded with many alternatives. Customer retention rates vary by industry.

For example, in the retail industry, it is quite low compared to other industries. This is because of fierce competition, which offers shoppers many choices, which are not satisfied with the product or brand, or may find a cheaper option. When assessing retention rates, it is important to account for acquisition costs. If the cost of acquisition and the retention rate are relatively small, the difference can be small. If the acquisition cost is high, but the retention rate is low, the company is likely to have problems. They may change their marketing strategy if customer leads from specific media do not stay around during the cohort window.

Below, we utilized broadcasting to calculate the retention rate (in percentage) by dividing each column by cohort_size. Later, we will see how to read cohort analysis from the annotated heatmap. 

# Retention Rate Table
# cohort size for day 1 each cohort group
cohort_size = cohort_total.iloc[:,0]
# retention rate for each day across the cohort group
retention = cohort_total.divide(cohort_size ,axis = 0)
# convert our index from timestamp to date format
retention.index = retention.index.date
# round the result to 3 digits (1 decimal point)
retention.round(3) * 100

Now, how do we infer from the triangular chart? It might look a little overwhelming at first, but we will see at two red arrows in the x and y axis to help us understand at the chart better. This chart has two main benefits:

Retention over user lifetime(depicted by horizontal arrow): will tell the long term relationship of any given cohort group. In the figure above, we are seeing at the cohorts activity (purchase) month-over-month. For each cohort we can see how many are coming back and how strong that cohort is. If we take the Dec 2010 cohort group,for example, we see how many people came to the site purchased in month 1, month 2, month 3 ...etc. In doing so we are following that cohort through their lifetime.

Retention over product lifetime(depicted by the vertical arrow): First thing on the left-hand side we are grouping customers by their purchase month starting Dec 2010 through Dec 2011. Here, we can compare different cohorts (group of customers) at the same stage in their life cycle. That would be looking down from top to bottom on the chart. For all the cohorts, what percentage are coming back to make purchases, say 3 months after their first purchase?

From the above chart we notice that fewer users stick around as time goes on. Also, we can see that Dec 2010 cohorts is the strongest. This will help us ask specific questions to that cohort group to know what is driving the customers to return back compared to other cohort groups after Jan 2011.
